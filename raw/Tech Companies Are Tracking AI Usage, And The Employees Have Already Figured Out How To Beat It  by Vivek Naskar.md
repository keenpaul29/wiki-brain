## Tech Companies Are Tracking AI Usage, And The Employees Have Already Figured Out How To Beat It

## Turns out, if you pressure people hard enough to hit a number, they will hit the number.

a11y-light · May 20, 2026 (Updated: May 20, 2026) · Free: No

There is a principle in economics that has been around since 1975, when British economist *[Charles Goodhart](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charles_Goodhart)* wrote about monetary policy in the UK.

> *The core idea is simple and a little brutal, when a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure.*

Nobody much cared about it outside academic circles for decades. Then Big Tech decided to build AI leaderboards, and suddenly *Goodhart* started looking like a prophet.

I have watched a lot of questionable management decisions come out of the tech industry over the past few years, having worked in the industry more than a decade now, but this one deserves its own category.

*Amazon*, *Meta*, and *Microsoft* spent the better part of the last year building internal dashboards to track how much their employees use AI tools. Yes, you read that right.

The stated logic was reasonable enough. More usage means more productivity gains, and executives wanted proof those gains were real before they had to explain the infrastructure bills to investors. What they got instead was a masterclass in how to destroy a metric by caring about it too much.

### Amazon Enforces Employees to use AI

Amazon set a target requiring more than 80% of its developers to use AI tools every single week. It tracked consumption on internal leaderboards and created an environment where the pressure to show up on the right side of that number was relentless.

One employee described the atmosphere as carrying *"perverse incentives."* Amazon officially told workers that AI usage statistics would not factor into performance evaluations.

Multiple employees said they did not believe that, and honestly, given everything else that has come out of corporate lately, I do not blame them for being skeptical.

So engineers did what people do when their career depends on a number. They found ways to move the number.

### Employees found a Hack to Cheat this

The behavior that emerged from all this pressure has now been given its own name in tech circles: *[tokenmaxxing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Token_maxxing)*.

The idea is exactly as cynical as it sounds. If you are being judged on how many AI tokens you consume, you find ways to run up the count regardless of whether the work actually needed doing.

At Amazon, some employees turned to *MeshClaw*, an in-house agent platform capable of initiating code deployments, triaging emails, and interacting with *Slack*.

Rather than using it for genuine tasks, workers ran it specifically to inflate their token consumption scores.

Meta and Microsoft had similar dynamics surface around the same time. Meta's internal AI usage leaderboard lasted only days after it was publicly reported before the company quietly pulled it.

Amazon has since restricted visibility of team-wide usage statistics, though anyone who thinks the underlying pressure disappeared along with the dashboard is being optimistic.

### Goodhart's Law at its Core

This is *Goodhart's Law* running at $700 billion scale.

Token consumption was never a measure of productivity. It was a proxy, a shortcut that looked measurable on a dashboard and gave executives something to point at during all-hands meetings.

The moment it became a target tied to people's careers, the only rational thing to do was game it. That is not a failure of individual ethics. That is exactly what the law predicts will happen, every single time, without exception.

### This is Where it Gets Expensive

The reason this is not just an embarrassing HR story is that the faked numbers did not stay inside the building.

For two years, every major hyperscaler has been telling investors the same story, AI adoption is accelerating, demand for compute is insatiable, and the infrastructure spending is justified by real, growing usage.

[Amazon, Microsoft, Google, and Meta together are tracking between $700 billion and $725 billion in capital expenditure for 2026](https://finance.yahoo.com/sectors/technology/articles/google-microsoft-meta-amazon-capex-131823436.html), up 77% from last year's already staggering $410 billion. That money goes into GPU orders, data center leases, and power contracts priced years in advance against demand signals that nobody has seriously questioned until now.

Scale that across thousands of engineers at Amazon, Meta, and Microsoft, and the adoption numbers executives are showing investors start to look a lot less reliable.

### Jensen Huang's Benchmark

*Nvidia* CEO *Jensen Huang* has made per-engineer token consumption something close to a moral benchmark.

Jensen Huang | By Raysonho @ Open Grid Scheduler / Scalable Grid Engine — Own work, CC0, [https://commons.wikimedia.org/w/index.php?curid=83647155](https://commons.wikimedia.org/w/index.php?curid=83647155)

He said at a public event that he would be "deeply alarmed" if a $500,000-a-year engineer was not consuming at least $250,000 worth of tokens annually, comparing engineers who skip AI tools to chip designers still working with paper and pencil.

I understand the point he was making, and I think he genuinely believes it. But that framing lands very differently when you know engineers across the industry were being pressured to hit token targets regardless of whether they had any real use for them. Every inflated token is real GPU time.

### What Comes Next

*Angie Jones*, formerly VP of engineering for AI tools at *Block*, has said she expects the industry to pivot toward measuring efficient token usage rather than raw volume.

That is a more honest framing, but getting there requires companies to admit their current metrics are broken, which is a harder conversation when the stock is up.

Meta pulled its leaderboard within days. Amazon buried the usage data shortly after. Draw your own conclusions.

### Closing Thoughts

Tokenmaxxing is what happens when you measure the wrong thing and tie people's livelihoods to the number.

I find it difficult to be too hard on the engineers who gamed the metric, because the alternative was watching their performance reviews suffer while colleagues who played along moved ahead.

Goodhart figured this out in 1975. The insight was not complicated then, and it is not complicated now. The only difference this time is the price tag.
