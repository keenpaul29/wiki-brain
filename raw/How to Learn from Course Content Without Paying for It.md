This is something I have figured out along the way when I was really tempted to buy a course. Initially, I used to buy courses based on the hours of content provided. Somehow, in my head, I felt like I was getting my money’s worth.

I used plenty of courses in my early days, which helped me build a mental model of how the pieces fit together. However, I never actually learned to code by following tutorials. The little coding I did learn came from practicing coding problems (loops, if-else). I had zero skills in searching the internet, and GitHub codebases were too advanced for me to understand. I also had no idea about real-world problems. My default approach was to go for courses.

Courses are also very appealing and feel like a shortcut — completing a “real-world project” in 18–20 hours seems effortless to a beginner. They are designed to impress the buyer.

Now, I take a look at the course curriculum, copy the titles, and follow them like a roadmap. There are plenty of resources for someone willing to learn. One thing a course provides is structure. The titles give me just enough insight to research those topics.

Without structure, reading thousands of articles left and right does no good. A structure helps separate what I know from what I don’t know. I can take a glimpse at an article and decide if I know it or not. But a video is a black box, leaving me wondering if I will find something valuable — unless it is specified at the start of the video.

This is something I miss when reading articles — there is no structure. I would be willing to pay for a course that consists solely of reading materials following a structured approach, combining both practical and theoretical knowledge. Books are valuable, but sometimes they delve too deeply into theoretical concepts without providing many practical use cases.

With that being said, there are a few good reasons to buy a course.

1. If I want to learn about something I know nothing about, small courses (5–6 hours) that cover the breadth of topics in that field are just enough for me to understand if it’s something I find interesting.
2. A course that explains complex theoretical topics like OS, databases, architecture, etc.
3. A course that explains how to use a tool like Excel, Notion, Slack, etc.
