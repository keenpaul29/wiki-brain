### How Do I Know I'm a 10× Dev? Work Experience

Back when I was working full-time, I found that I was consistently 5–10× faster than all of my "mid-level" or "senior" SWE colleagues during my last 5+ years of full-stack software engineering, and that was before AI.

As a former SWE hiring manager, I categorize SWEs based on productivity: 1× junior, 2× senior, 4× "staff", 10× "10× dev" or "lead."

That's always been how it works — and I started working professionally as an SWE way back in 2005.

Even devs who have worked for "big" companies are often "2× devs," yet "10× devs" are very real.

I would assume 100% of candidates in today's job market would use LLMs for applying to jobs, unless you specifically and clearly state, "No AI usage allowed in your application."

As a candidate, I know response rates to applicants are now just too low to justify handwriting cover letters from scratch like I used to.

### Why Some SWEs Are OK With LLMs' Slop Secretions

But there are many applicants who won't be bothered if the LLM lies on their behalf.

That's the "slop is OK with me" SWE crowd.

I'd never allow that, just like I'd never commit LLM-generated code to a codebase unless I reviewed it first.

The problem with AI usage is that LLMs secrete slop, and slop sucks.

No one wants AI-generated slop, not code, not art, not music, not anything.

Lazy, AI-generated slop is soulless, low-quality, worthless junk.

Quality, heart, and soul are the most important things.

My personal philosophy that I include in my core context engineering document is ["Quality Rules Everything Around Me" (QREAM)](https://medium.com/career-programming/why-i-hate-the-idea-of-mvp-and-what-i-do-instead-as-an-indie-game-dev-35806bb0f7b3).

In other words, I think LLMs should only be used to increase quality, not as a substitute for effort.

If I were using LLMs to avoid work, secrete slop, or be lazy without putting in any effort, I wouldn't call myself a professional 10× developer.

Like all of my usage of LLMs, I manually verify, check, and edit all of the AI-generated text for accuracy.

### I Increase Quality by Dictating Prompts to LLMs

I do dictate everything because I dictate 3× faster (5,000 words per hour measured for original composition) than typing (1,400 words per hour).

I'll proofread manually for any automatic speech recognition (ASR) mistakes.

I dictate 100% of the words on my Medium blog (and have published daily all year) without any AI-generated text.

I use LLMs on my Medium blog 100% of the time, but ONLY to improve quality: for proofreading and to create Python data science visualizations as visual aids.

Nevertheless, I'd never go back to architecting, engineering, or coding without LLMs.

Why?

It's another ~120%–250%\* speed boost on top of my 10× baseline, meaning I have more time to focus on improving quality.

*\*Author's note: This is a ballpark LLM-assisted SWE speed estimate for SWEs reviewing code and avoiding slop, based on my own personal experience.*

### Cheap LLMs Don't Demonstrate Abstract Reasoning

I also dictate to LLMs (via [Handy](https://medium.com/according-to-context/this-0-local-ai-dictation-tool-saves-me-2-or-more-hours-every-day-588c0664f0a1) and [Whisper](https://medium.com/according-to-context/how-i-3x-ed-my-productivity-with-0-local-ai-without-using-generative-ai-242f0014f1e7)) and have them generate my game design documents (GDDs) based exactly on my specifications, always human-verifying the result, for the same reason: to improve quality.

Without LLMs, I would probably skip writing detailed GDDs, but now I have 5,000+ to 50,000+ words of written documentation on each of my in-progress games.

State-of-the-art (SOTA) LLMs like Gemini Deep Think can definitely understand (not in a conscious way, but through abstract reasoning) logical concepts like state machine architecture.

There's a world of difference between iteratively prompting 1,000+ words (dictating for 10–15 minutes at a time) to a $250/month LLM (Deep Think) to build a living document (like a GDD) compared to sending 7 words of "write a flappy bird game for me" to a free/cheap/local LLM.

Cheap LLMs definitely never develop any "understanding" — it took me 10,000+ tokens of context engineering and upgrading to a far more expensive LLM (Deep Think) to get the LLM to consistently output code and GDD updates that demonstrate understanding.

But LLMs do demonstrate capability for abstract reasoning.

I spend far more time planning and creating architecture and functional specifications than I do having the LLM generate code, because LLMs are generally bad at engineering (and even worse at architecture) except with clear direction.

### As a 10× SWE, I Own Architecture & Do Lots of QA

I find that doing thorough quality assurance (QA) followed by thorough code review are necessary steps.

Some SWEs skip those steps when using AI and are generating low-quality, bug-filled code faster than ever.

I take the opposite approach and only ever use LLMs iteratively to spend more time catching bugs and other mistakes, in other words to improve quality.

I build everything I can out of state machines (also called statecharts) to eliminate a whole class of bugs.

Generally speaking, the more freedom I have to choose the architectural approach, the more I'll rely on deterministic state machines.

Passing off AI-generated, buggy crap as a game won't work, because it won't generate positive reviews.

### Wrapping Up: How I Use LLMs as an Indie Game Dev

LLMs are only useful to us indie game devs if they allow us to fix all the bugs and deliver a premium, world-class product beyond what we'd achieve with our budgets otherwise.

At the same time, I think LLMs allow us (small teams of indie game devs, designers, artists, directors, etc.) to publish high-quality games — games good enough to sell — in a way like never before.

They allow us to develop our game ideas faster and to a higher quality, as long as we don't let LLMs think for us or suck out our human souls.

I also love doing QA, specifically catching and eliminating all bugs, because I know bugs = bad reviews, and bad reviews = games that don't sell.

I'm always optimizing for player experience and quality = good reviews = games that sell.
